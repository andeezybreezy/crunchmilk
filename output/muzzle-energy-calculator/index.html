Muzzle Energy Calculator

Calculate bullet muzzle energy, momentum, and power factor from velocity and weight

What the Muzzle Energy Calculator Does

This calculator turns two numbers you can read off an ammo box and a chronograph — bullet weight in grains and muzzle velocity in feet per second — into muzzle energy in foot-pounds, the standard way American shooters and hunters compare how hard a load hits. Alongside energy it also reports momentum, USPSA Power Factor, and the Taylor Knockout index, so a single set of inputs lets you size up a load from several angles. It's built for handloaders dialing in a charge, hunters checking a cartridge against game thresholds, and competitors confirming their Power Factor before a match.

A Worked Example

Say you're shooting a classic 55 gr .223 varmint load and your chronograph reads 3,000 fps. Square the velocity: 3,000² = 9,000,000. Multiply by the 55 gr bullet weight: 55 × 9,000,000 = 495,000,000. Divide by the 450,240 constant: 495,000,000 ÷ 450,240 ≈ 1,099 ft-lbs. That single figure lets you line the load up against anything else in your safe — a 9mm carry round at roughly 360 ft-lbs, or a .30-06 elk load north of 2,900 ft-lbs — on exactly the same scale.

Why Velocity Beats Weight

The formula divides bullet weight times velocity-squared by 450,240, and that squared term is the whole story. Weight scales energy one-for-one, but velocity scales it by the square: add 10% to the weight and you add 10% to the energy, but add 10% to the velocity and energy climbs about 21%. That's why a slim, fast bullet can carry more punch than a heavier, slower one, and why handloaders chasing energy usually push velocity first. It's also why chronographing your own loads matters — a 100 fps difference between advertised and actual velocity moves the energy figure more than most people expect.

How to Use This Calculator

  1. Enter Your Bullet Weight (grains): Read the bullet weight in grains from the ammo box or your load data and type it in. Common values run from 40 gr for small varmint bullets up to 220 gr or more for heavy rifle loads.
  2. Enter Your Muzzle Velocity (fps): Enter velocity in feet per second. A chronograph reading from your own barrel is best, since real velocity often trails the box figure by 50–150 fps depending on barrel length and temperature.
  3. Enter Your Bullet Diameter (inches): Type the bullet diameter in inches — 0.355 for 9mm, 0.308 for most .30-caliber rifles, 0.224 for .223/5.56. This feeds the Taylor Knockout index; energy and Power Factor don't use it.
  4. Click Calculate: Hit Calculate to square the velocity, apply the formulas, and return all four metrics at once. Everything runs in your browser.
  5. Compare Your Results: Read your muzzle energy in foot-pounds and check it against the load you're comparing to, or against the deer (~1,000 ft-lbs) and elk (~1,500+ ft-lbs) thresholds for your expected range.

How It Works

Muzzle energy is the kinetic energy a bullet carries as it leaves the barrel, and it comes down to two numbers you already know: how heavy the bullet is and how fast it's moving. This calculator runs those numbers through the standard ballistics formulas so you can compare loads on an even footing.

The basic rule:

  • Energy (ft-lbs) = (Weight (gr) × Velocity²) ÷ 450,240
  • Momentum = (Weight ÷ 7000) × Velocity
  • Power Factor = (Weight × Velocity) ÷ 1,000
  • Taylor KO = (Weight × Velocity × Diameter) ÷ 7,000

The 450,240 constant folds together the conversion from grains to pounds (÷7,000) and the gravitational constant that turns weight into mass, so you can plug in the numbers a chronograph and a load manual actually give you. Chronograph velocity varies shot to shot, so treat the energy figure as the average for that string, not an exact single-shot value.

Tips & Considerations

  • Chronograph your own loads before trusting an energy figure — advertised velocities are often measured in longer test barrels and can overstate what your rifle actually produces by 50–150 fps.
  • Energy thresholds for game apply at the point of impact, not the muzzle, so account for how much velocity your load sheds over the distance you actually shoot.
  • When two loads make similar energy, the faster, lighter one usually shoots flatter, while the heavier, slower one penetrates deeper and bucks wind better — energy alone won't tell you which to pick.
  • Because velocity is squared, small velocity gains from a hotter charge move energy more than an equivalent bump in bullet weight — worth remembering when working up a load.
  • Don't read muzzle energy as a stand-in for stopping power; bullet construction, expansion, and shot placement decide terminal performance, and no single number captures all three.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is muzzle energy?

Muzzle energy is the kinetic energy of a bullet at the instant it exits the barrel, measured in foot-pounds (ft-lbs) in the United States. It represents the total energy available to do work on the target — penetration, expansion, and tissue disruption all draw from this budget. Energy is highest at the muzzle and drops continuously downrange as the bullet slows, so a load rated at 1,500 ft-lbs at the muzzle might carry only 900 ft-lbs at 200 yards.

Why is velocity squared in the muzzle energy formula?

Kinetic energy is defined as ½ × mass × velocity², so speed enters the equation as a square while weight enters only linearly. Practically, that means velocity is the dominant lever: doubling bullet weight doubles energy, but doubling velocity quadruples it. This is why a light, fast varmint bullet can out-punch a heavier, slower one. A 55 gr bullet at 3,000 fps makes (55 × 3000²) ÷ 450,240 ≈ 1,099 ft-lbs, while a 110 gr bullet at the same 3,000 fps makes about 2,199 ft-lbs — but drop that 110 gr load to 1,500 fps and it falls to just 550 ft-lbs.

How do you calculate muzzle energy by hand?

Square the velocity in fps, multiply by the bullet weight in grains, then divide by 450,240. Worked example: a 55 gr bullet at 3,000 fps gives 3000² = 9,000,000; times 55 = 495,000,000; divided by 450,240 ≈ 1,099 ft-lbs. The only two inputs that matter are weight (from the box or load manual) and velocity (from a chronograph or published data).

How much muzzle energy do you need for deer?

A widely cited rule of thumb is about 1,000 ft-lbs of energy delivered at the point of impact for deer-sized game, though many states set no hard energy floor and shot placement matters far more than any single number. Common deer cartridges clear this easily at the muzzle: a .243 Win (100 gr at ~2,960 fps) makes roughly 1,945 ft-lbs, and a .30-30 (150 gr at ~2,390 fps) makes about 1,900 ft-lbs. Remember the threshold applies at impact, not the muzzle, so factor in how much energy the load retains at your expected range.

How much muzzle energy do you need for elk?

Elk are far larger and tougher than deer, and the commonly recommended minimum is around 1,500 ft-lbs at impact, with many hunters preferring 2,000 ft-lbs or more for margin. A .30-06 (180 gr at ~2,700 fps) produces roughly 2,913 ft-lbs at the muzzle, and a .300 Win Mag pushes well past 3,500 ft-lbs. Because energy bleeds off with distance, a load that meets the elk threshold at the muzzle may fall short at long range — check retained energy at your maximum shooting distance, not just at the barrel.

What is Power Factor and how does it differ from energy?

Power Factor = (Bullet Weight × Velocity) ÷ 1,000, and it is a momentum-based figure used in USPSA and IPSC competition rather than an energy figure. Because it uses velocity to the first power (not squared), it rewards heavy, slower bullets more than the energy formula does. Major scoring requires 165+ PF and Minor requires 125+ PF. A 124 gr 9mm at 1,150 fps makes a PF of about 143 — comfortably Minor, but short of Major.
