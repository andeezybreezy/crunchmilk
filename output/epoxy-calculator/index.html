How It Works

This calculator uses simple geometry and the known density of mixed epoxy resin to give you accurate material estimates for any project.

The basic rule:

  • Calculate the volume of your project area in cubic inches (length × width × depth for rectangles, or π × r² × depth for circles)
  • Convert cubic inches to fluid ounces (1 cubic inch = 0.554 fl oz)
  • Apply your selected waste factor (10% recommended to account for mixing, drips, and container residue)
  • Split the total volume into resin and hardener based on your mix ratio (1:1 or 2:1)
  • Always measure by volume, not weight — resin and hardener have different densities
  • For deep pours over 1/4 inch, use a deep-pour / slow-cure epoxy to prevent overheating

These calculations give you the minimum amount needed. When in doubt, round up — running short mid-pour is far worse than having a little extra. Most epoxy has a shelf life of 12+ months, so leftover material won't go to waste.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much epoxy do I need per square foot?

For a standard 1/8-inch thick coating, you need approximately 10 fluid ounces (about 1.25 cups) of mixed epoxy per square foot. For a thinner seal coat at 1/16 inch, you'll need about 5 oz per square foot. For deep pours, multiply accordingly — a 1-inch deep pour over one square foot requires about 80 oz (0.6 gallons) of mixed epoxy.

What's the difference between 1:1 and 2:1 epoxy?

The ratio refers to how much resin vs. hardener you mix. A 1:1 system uses equal parts resin and hardener by volume — this is the most common type sold at hardware stores and is easiest to measure. A 2:1 system uses twice as much resin as hardener. 2:1 systems often offer better UV resistance and clarity, and are common in marine and high-performance applications. Always follow the manufacturer's specified ratio exactly — incorrect ratios cause incomplete curing.

How thick should an epoxy coating be?

For a basic protective coating on a table top or countertop, 1/8 inch (about 3mm) is standard. Most coating epoxies are designed to self-level at this thickness. You can apply multiple thin coats (1/16 inch each) for more control. For a bar-top finish with embedded objects, 1/4 inch is common. For river tables and casting, depths range from 1 to 3 inches depending on your epoxy's maximum pour depth.

Can I pour deep epoxy all at once?

It depends on the epoxy type. Standard table-top / coating epoxy should NOT be poured deeper than 1/4 inch at once — it generates too much heat (exothermic reaction) and can crack, yellow, or warp your project. Deep-pour epoxy is specially formulated with slower cure times to manage heat, and can typically handle 1 to 2 inches per pour. Some deep-pour formulas allow up to 4 inches. Always check your product's data sheet for maximum pour depth.

How do I prevent bubbles in epoxy?

Bubbles are the most common epoxy problem. To minimize them: (1) Warm your resin to 75-85°F before mixing — warm resin is thinner and releases bubbles faster. (2) Mix slowly and deliberately for the full recommended time (usually 3-5 minutes) to avoid whipping in air. (3) Apply a thin seal coat first and let it tack up before your flood coat — this seals porous wood and prevents off-gassing. (4) Use a heat gun or propane torch lightly across the surface after pouring to pop surface bubbles. (5) Pour in a room that's 70-80°F with low humidity.

How long does epoxy take to cure?

Cure times vary by product and temperature. Most coating epoxies are tack-free in 12-24 hours and reach a light-use cure in 48-72 hours. Full mechanical cure (safe for heavy use, placing hot items, etc.) typically takes 7 days at 70°F. Deep-pour epoxies cure more slowly by design — expect 24-48 hours to demold and 5-7 days for full cure. Cold temperatures slow curing dramatically; try to maintain at least 70°F during the entire cure period.
