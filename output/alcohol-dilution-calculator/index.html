How to Convert Oven Recipes to Air Fryer

Diluting spirits with water lowers the alcohol by volume (ABV) percentage. The key formula comes from Pearson's square, which calculates how much water (0% ABV) to add to reach a target concentration.

The basic rule:

  • Formula: Water to add = Current Volume × (Current ABV / Target ABV − 1)
  • Proof vs ABV: US Proof = ABV × 2. So 80 proof = 40% ABV.
  • This formula assumes ideal mixing. In practice, alcohol + water has slight volume contraction (~3-5%), so the actual final volume is slightly less than calculated.

Use distilled or filtered water for best results. Add water slowly and stir. For spirits, let the diluted product rest for at least 24-48 hours before tasting, as the flavor integrates over time. Temperature affects the reading — standard is 60°F (15.6°C).

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Pearson's square?

Pearson's square is a simple method for calculating mixing ratios to achieve a target concentration. For alcohol dilution, it compares the current ABV and target ABV to determine the ratio of spirit to water needed. The formula simplifies to: water = volume × (current_ABV / target_ABV − 1).

Does mixing alcohol and water change the total volume?

Yes, slightly. When ethanol and water mix, the total volume contracts by about 3-5% compared to the sum of the individual volumes. This is due to hydrogen bonding between molecules. For home use, this difference is negligible. Professional distillers account for it.

Why do distillers proof down spirits?

Spirits come off the still at very high proof (often 130-160+ proof). Most spirits are bottled at 80-100 proof. Proofing down with water makes the spirit drinkable, brings out flavor complexity, and meets legal bottling requirements.

Can I use tap water to dilute spirits?

Use distilled or carbon-filtered water. Tap water may contain chlorine, minerals, or off-flavors that can affect the taste of your spirits. Reverse-osmosis water also works well. Avoid mineral water unless you specifically want those mineral characteristics.

How do I measure ABV at home?

Use an alcoholmeter (also called a proof hydrometer). Float it in a sample of your spirit at 60°F (15.6°C) and read the scale where it meets the liquid surface. Digital refractometers also work. Sugar content affects readings, so use a hydrometer for sugared spirits.

Can I use this for diluting wine must or mead?

Yes, the same formula applies. When diluting wine must or mead, you are lowering the potential alcohol by diluting the sugar concentration. Be aware that dilution also affects flavor intensity, acidity, and body.
