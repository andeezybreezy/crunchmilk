How to Convert Oven Recipes to Air Fryer

This calculator generates a composite wildfire risk score (0-100) by weighting six key factors that determine a property's vulnerability to wildfire damage.

The basic rule:

  • Vegetation type contributes 30% of the score — dense dry brush and conifers score highest risk
  • Terrain slope contributes 20% — fire travels faster uphill, doubling speed for every 20% increase in slope
  • Climate zone contributes 15% — arid and drought-prone regions have higher ignition and spread risk
  • Defensible space contributes 15% — CAL FIRE recommends 100 feet of defensible space in high-risk zones
  • Roof material contributes 10% — ember exposure is the leading cause of structure ignition during wildfires
  • Road access contributes 10% — affects both evacuation safety and firefighter response time

This is an educational risk estimate. For official risk assessments, consult your local fire department, CAL FIRE hazard maps, or a certified wildfire risk assessor. Insurance companies use proprietary models that may weight factors differently.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does Nevada handle this differently?

Nevada has its own specific rules, rates, and limits that may differ significantly from federal guidelines or other states. This calculator uses Nevada-specific data where available. Always verify with a local professional for important decisions.

What is a good wildfire risk score?

Scores under 30 indicate low risk, 30-50 moderate risk, 50-70 high risk, and above 70 extreme risk. Most properties in wildland-urban interface areas score 40-70. The goal is to reduce controllable factors like defensible space and building materials.

How much defensible space do I need?

CAL FIRE requires 100 feet of defensible space around structures in State Responsibility Areas. Zone 1 (0-30 feet) should be lean, clean, and green with no combustible material. Zone 2 (30-100 feet) should have reduced and spaced vegetation. Some communities require up to 200 feet.

Does wildfire risk affect my insurance?

Yes, significantly. Homes in high-risk areas may face premiums 2-5x higher than low-risk homes, or may be dropped by insurers entirely. California's FAIR Plan provides last-resort coverage. Demonstrating defensible space and fire-resistant materials can help reduce premiums.

What roof material is best for wildfire?

Class A rated roofing (metal, concrete tile, clay tile, slate) offers the best fire protection. Asphalt composition shingles are Class A or B. Wood shake and shingle roofs are the most vulnerable. Replacing a wood shake roof is one of the most impactful mitigation steps.

How does slope affect wildfire spread?

Fire moves faster uphill because flames preheat upslope fuels. For every 10-degree increase in slope, fire spread rate roughly doubles. A fire on a 30% slope can move 2-4 times faster than on flat ground. Homes on ridgetops and steep hillsides face elevated risk from below.
