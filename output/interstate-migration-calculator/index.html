Frequently Asked Questions

Which states have no income tax?

Nine states have no state income tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (dividends/interest only), South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. These states generate revenue through other taxes like sales tax, property tax, or severance taxes.

How much does it cost to move interstate?

A full-service interstate move for a 2-3 bedroom home typically costs $3,000-$7,500 depending on distance. Cross-country moves (2,000+ miles) can reach $8,000-$15,000. DIY truck rentals run $1,500-$4,000 for the same distances.

Do I have to pay income tax in both states when I move?

Yes, in most cases. You'll owe taxes to your origin state for income earned before the move and to your destination state for income earned after. Most states have part-year resident filing. Some state pairs have reciprocity agreements.

How does cost of living vary between states?

Cost of living varies dramatically — Hawaii and California are 80-90% above the national average, while Mississippi and Oklahoma are 15-20% below. Housing is typically the biggest factor, followed by healthcare, transportation, and food.

What hidden costs come with interstate moves?

Common hidden costs include: new state vehicle registration and inspection ($50-$500), updated driver's license ($15-$90), new professional license fees, lease-breaking penalties, utility deposits ($200-$500), and temporary housing if there's a gap between moves.

Should I move to a no-income-tax state to save money?

Not necessarily. No-income-tax states often have higher property taxes (Texas), higher sales taxes (Tennessee), or higher cost of living (Washington).
