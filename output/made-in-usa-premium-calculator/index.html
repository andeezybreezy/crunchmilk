How It Works

This calculator estimates the price premium for American-made products using category-specific markup data from retail price surveys, manufacturing cost analysis, and consumer reports.

The basic rule:

  • Made in USA premiums vary widely by category — clothing can be 40-100% more, while food is often only 5-20% more
  • Durability offsets: American-made tools and furniture often last significantly longer, reducing effective cost per year
  • Electronics have the highest premiums (80-200%) because almost all consumer electronics manufacturing is overseas
  • The premium reflects both higher US labor costs and smaller production scale for domestic manufacturers

Buying American involves trade-offs beyond price: supporting domestic jobs, shorter supply chains, potentially higher quality, and reduced environmental impact from shipping. This calculator helps quantify the price side of that decision.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are Made in USA products more expensive?

Three main factors: (1) US labor costs are 5-20x higher than in countries like China, Vietnam, or Bangladesh. (2) Domestic manufacturers often produce at smaller scale, losing the volume discounts that massive overseas factories achieve. (3) US environmental and safety regulations add compliance costs. However, these factors also often result in higher quality and better working conditions.

Are American-made products actually better quality?

It depends on the category. For tools, furniture, and workwear, US-made products often are measurably more durable. For electronics, the difference is minimal since components are largely the same globally. For clothing, US-made often uses better materials and construction. The quality premium is real but varies — it is not a universal rule.

Which categories are worth paying the USA premium?

Best value for domestic: tools and hardware (last decades), furniture (solid wood vs particle board), work boots and outerwear (durability), and food (freshness, fewer preservatives). Worst value: consumer electronics (minimal quality difference, huge price gap) and basic commodity goods (paper products, generic supplies).

How do tariffs affect the Made in USA premium?

Tariffs on imported goods effectively reduce the premium by making imports more expensive. A 25% tariff on Chinese furniture, for example, would narrow the USA premium from ~60% to ~28%. Recent tariff increases have made some domestic products more price-competitive than they have been in decades.
