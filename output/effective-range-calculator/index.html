How It Works

Effective range is determined by two factors: the distance at which bullet energy drops below the threshold for a clean ethical kill, and the distance at which your accuracy can no longer reliably hit the vital zone. The shorter of these two distances is your true maximum effective range.

The basic rule:

  • Energy threshold depends on game: 1,000 ft-lbs for deer, 1,500 ft-lbs for elk, 200 ft-lbs for varmint
  • Accuracy range = vital zone size ÷ (shooter MOA × 1.047) × 100 yards
  • Your effective range is the shorter of the energy range and the accuracy range

These are theoretical maximums under ideal conditions. Real-world factors like wind, shooting position, adrenaline, and animal movement reduce practical range. Most ethical hunters limit themselves well below their theoretical maximum.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the minimum energy needed to ethically kill a deer?

The widely accepted minimum is 1,000 ft-lbs of energy at impact for deer-sized game. This ensures reliable bullet expansion and sufficient penetration for a quick, humane kill. Some experts recommend even more — 1,200 ft-lbs — for marginal shot angles.

Why does accuracy matter for effective range?

A bullet with enough energy is useless if you can't place it in the vital zone. If your rifle shoots 2 MOA groups, that's a 10-inch spread at 500 yards. On a deer with an 8-inch vital zone, your chances of a clean hit are poor at that distance.

What MOA accuracy should I use for this calculator?

Use your actual group size from a field position, not a bench rest. Most hunters shoot 2-4 MOA from field positions. If you only shoot from a bench, add 1-2 MOA for real-world conditions. Be honest — a wounded animal suffers from optimistic range estimates.

Is 6.5 Creedmoor better for long range than .308?

The 6.5 Creedmoor has a significantly higher ballistic coefficient, meaning it retains energy and velocity better at long range. A 6.5CM typically maintains 1,000+ ft-lbs past 700 yards while the .308 drops below that threshold around 500-600 yards.

What is the effective range of .223 Remington for deer?

The .223 Remington drops below the 1,000 ft-lbs threshold very quickly — typically by 100 yards. Most wildlife agencies don't recommend .223 for deer. Where it's legal, ethical shots are generally limited to 100 yards or less with proper bullet selection.

How does wind affect effective range?

Wind is often the true limiting factor for long-range shooting. A 10 mph crosswind can push a .308 bullet over 30 inches at 500 yards. If you can't accurately read and compensate for wind, your practical effective range is much shorter than the energy limit.
