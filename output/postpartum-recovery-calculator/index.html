How It Works

Postpartum recovery follows a general timeline, though every person's experience is unique. Recovery time depends on delivery type, complications, and individual health factors.

The basic rule:

  • Vaginal delivery: general recovery 6 weeks, full recovery 6–12 months
  • C-section: incision healing 6–8 weeks, full recovery 8–12+ months
  • Postpartum bleeding (lochia) typically lasts 2–6 weeks
  • Exercise clearance: 6 weeks (vaginal) or 8 weeks (C-section) with provider approval

This timeline provides general guidance. Always follow your healthcare provider's specific instructions, especially regarding activity restrictions, lifting limits, and returning to exercise. Contact your provider immediately if you experience heavy bleeding, fever, severe pain, or signs of postpartum depression.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does postpartum recovery take?

Basic recovery takes about 6 weeks for vaginal delivery and 8 weeks for C-section. However, full physical recovery — including pelvic floor healing, core strength, and hormonal stabilization — typically takes 6-12 months. Some effects of pregnancy and birth last even longer.

When can I exercise after giving birth?

Most providers clear gentle walking immediately. For vaginal birth, moderate exercise is typically approved at 6 weeks. For C-section, wait 8 weeks or until your provider clears you. Start gradually with low-impact activities and pelvic floor exercises before returning to high-intensity workouts.

How long does postpartum bleeding last?

Postpartum bleeding (lochia) typically lasts 2-6 weeks. It starts heavy and red, gradually becoming lighter and changing to pink, then brown, then yellowish-white. If bleeding increases after slowing down, you may be overdoing activity. Contact your provider if you soak a pad in an hour.

When does a C-section incision fully heal?

The external incision typically closes within 2-3 weeks and stitches/staples are removed at 1-2 weeks. The deeper tissue layers take 6-8 weeks to heal. The internal scar continues to strengthen for up to 12 months. Numbness around the incision may last 6-12+ months.

When should I call my doctor postpartum?

Call immediately for: heavy bleeding (soaking a pad per hour), fever over 100.4°F, severe abdominal pain, foul-smelling discharge, difficulty breathing, chest pain, persistent headache or vision changes, thoughts of self-harm, or redness/swelling at C-section incision.

When can I drive after a C-section?

Most providers recommend waiting 2-4 weeks after a C-section before driving. You should be off narcotic pain medication and able to brake quickly and comfortably. Check with your insurance company as well — some policies have specific restrictions after surgery.
