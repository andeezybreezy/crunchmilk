How It Works

This bullet drop calculator uses a simplified flat-fire ballistic model with drag to estimate bullet trajectory at various distances. It accounts for gravity drop, air resistance via the G1 ballistic coefficient, and optional crosswind drift.

The basic rule:

  • Select a preset caliber or enter custom ballistic data (muzzle velocity, bullet weight, BC)
  • The calculator computes trajectory at 50-yard increments from 100 to 1000 yards
  • Drop, remaining velocity, energy, and holdover (MOA and MIL) are displayed for each range

Results are approximations for standard sea-level conditions. Real-world performance varies with altitude, temperature, humidity, and barrel length. Always confirm zero at the range before relying on calculated holdovers.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is this bullet drop calculator?

This calculator uses a simplified flat-fire drag model that is reasonably accurate out to moderate ranges for most rifle cartridges. For precision long-range work beyond 600 yards, dedicated ballistic software accounting for Coriolis effect, spin drift, and atmospheric conditions will be more precise.

What is ballistic coefficient (BC)?

Ballistic coefficient measures a bullet's ability to overcome air resistance. A higher BC means the bullet retains velocity and energy better over distance. BC values are specific to each bullet design and weight. G1 is the most common reference standard.

What is the difference between MOA and MIL holdover?

MOA (Minute of Angle) equals approximately 1.047 inches at 100 yards. MIL (milliradian) equals approximately 3.6 inches at 100 yards. Most American scopes use MOA adjustments while many tactical and European scopes use MIL. Both measure the same angle, just in different units.

Why does my bullet drop so much past 500 yards?

Bullet drop increases exponentially with distance because gravity acts on the bullet for a longer time as it slows down. Air resistance continuously decelerates the bullet, so it spends more time in flight at longer ranges, allowing gravity to pull it further below the line of sight.

What zero range should I use?

A 100-yard zero is the most common for hunting rifles. Some shooters prefer a 200-yard zero for flatter trajectory at moderate ranges, accepting a slight high impact at 100 yards. For rimfire or short-range cartridges, a 50-yard zero is typical.

How does wind affect bullet trajectory?

A crosswind pushes the bullet sideways throughout its flight. The deflection increases with range and is worse for slower, lower-BC bullets. A 10 mph crosswind can push a .308 bullet over 10 inches at 300 yards and over 60 inches at 600 yards.
