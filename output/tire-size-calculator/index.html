How to Convert Oven Recipes to Air Fryer

Tire diameter is calculated from the metric tire size format (e.g. 225/60R16) using the section width, aspect ratio, and rim diameter.

The basic rule:

  • Sidewall height = section width (mm) × aspect ratio / 100
  • Overall diameter = (2 × sidewall height / 25.4) + rim diameter in inches
  • Circumference = π × diameter
  • Revolutions per mile = 63,360 ÷ circumference in inches
  • Speedometer error = ((new diameter − original diameter) / original diameter) × 100%

Most manufacturers recommend staying within 3% of the original tire diameter. Larger tires make your speedometer read slower than actual speed.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I read a tire size like 225/60R16?

225 is the section width in millimeters, 60 is the aspect ratio (sidewall height is 60% of width), R means radial construction, and 16 is the rim diameter in inches.

How does tire size affect the speedometer?

A larger tire covers more ground per revolution, so your speedometer will read lower than your actual speed. A 3% larger tire means at an indicated 60 mph, you're actually going about 61.8 mph.

What is the 3% rule for tire sizes?

Most vehicle manufacturers recommend staying within 3% of the original tire diameter. Going beyond this can affect speedometer accuracy, ABS, traction control, and may cause rubbing on fenders or suspension components.

Does tire size affect fuel economy?

Yes. Larger tires are heavier and have more rolling resistance, which can decrease fuel economy by 1-2 MPG. Wider tires also increase aerodynamic drag slightly. However, the effect is usually small within the 3% range.
