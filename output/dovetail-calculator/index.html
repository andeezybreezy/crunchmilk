How It Works

Dovetail joints are the hallmark of quality woodworking. Proper spacing ensures even, attractive joints that are structurally sound. This calculator divides your board width into evenly-spaced pins and tails.

The basic rule:

  • Layout starts with half-pins at each edge, then alternates tails and full pins
  • Number of pins = number of tails + 1 (including two half-pins)
  • Dovetail angle is typically 1:6 for softwood and 1:8 for hardwood
  • Joint depth equals the board thickness (the mating board's thickness)

Mark your dovetails from the narrow face (pin face). The tail board gets cut first, then used as a template to mark the pin board for a perfect fit. Leave your lines when cutting pins, split the line on tails.

Frequently Asked Questions

What dovetail ratio should I use?

Use 1:6 (about 14 degrees) for softwoods like pine, 1:7 for general purpose, and 1:8 (about 7 degrees) for hardwoods like oak and maple. Steeper angles (lower ratio) grip better in soft wood; shallower angles look more refined in hardwood.

How many tails should I use?

A common guideline is one tail per inch of board width. A 6-inch board might have 3-4 tails. Fewer, larger tails look bolder; more, smaller tails look more refined. There's no structural difference.

What are half-pins?

Half-pins are the triangular pieces at the top and bottom edges of the pin board. They prevent the joint from sliding apart and give a clean finished edge. They're typically 1/4 to 1/2 the width of a full tail.

Should I cut pins first or tails first?

Both methods work. Tails-first is traditional for hand-cut dovetails — cut the tails, then use the tail board to mark the pins. Pins-first is preferred by some for through dovetails. Choose the method you're most accurate with.

How tight should dovetails fit?

Aim for a fit that requires light mallet taps to assemble. Too tight and you'll split the wood; too loose and the joint is weak. The joint should go together with hand pressure and a few taps, not a hammer.
