How It Works

Boat fuel consumption depends on engine horsepower, fuel type, and throttle setting. The standard rule of thumb uses a simple HP-based formula.

The basic rule:

  • Gasoline engines: GPH = HP × 0.10 at full throttle
  • Diesel engines: GPH = HP × 0.055 at full throttle
  • Fuel needed = GPH × (distance / speed). Always add a 10% safety reserve

These are estimates based on rule-of-thumb calculations. Actual consumption varies with hull design, load, sea conditions, and engine efficiency. Always carry more fuel than calculated, especially offshore.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate boat fuel consumption?

For gasoline engines, multiply HP by 0.10 to get gallons per hour at full throttle. For diesel, multiply by 0.055. Then multiply GPH by the number of hours your trip will take (distance / speed). Always add a 10% safety reserve.

How much fuel does a 150 HP outboard use?

A 150 HP gasoline outboard at full throttle uses about 15 gallons per hour (150 × 0.10). At cruising speed (75% throttle), consumption drops to about 11.25 GPH. At trolling speed (50%), it's about 7.5 GPH.

What is the 1/3 fuel rule for boats?

The rule of thirds says: use 1/3 of your fuel to go out, 1/3 to come back, and keep 1/3 in reserve. This calculator uses a 10% reserve which is the minimum — the 1/3 rule is more conservative and recommended for offshore trips or unfamiliar waters.

How far can a boat go on a tank of gas?

Range = tank capacity × nautical MPG. For example, a boat with a 100-gallon tank getting 2 NMPG has a range of 200 nautical miles. At 75% throttle with a 150HP engine, that's about 100 gallons / 11.25 GPH × 20 knots = 178 nm.

Does speed affect fuel consumption?

Yes, dramatically. Most boats have a sweet spot around 60-75% throttle where fuel efficiency is best. At wide open throttle, fuel consumption can increase 50-100% while speed only increases 20-30%. Planing hulls are most efficient at or just above planing speed.

Is diesel more fuel efficient than gas for boats?

Yes. Diesel engines use roughly 55% less fuel per HP than gasoline engines (0.055 vs 0.10 GPH/HP). Diesel fuel also has more energy per gallon. However, diesel engines cost more upfront. For boats used frequently or over long distances, diesel saves significantly on fuel costs.
