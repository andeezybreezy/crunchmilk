How to Convert Oven Recipes to Air Fryer

Per diem rates are set by the General Services Administration (GSA) and determine how much federal employees and contractors can be reimbursed for travel expenses.

The basic rule:

  • Rates consist of two parts: lodging and M&IE (meals and incidental expenses)
  • Standard CONUS rate: $107 lodging + $59 M&IE = $166/day
  • High-cost cities: $200–$350+ lodging + $64–$79 M&IE
  • First and last travel days are reimbursed at 75% of M&IE

Many private employers also use GSA rates as a basis for their travel reimbursement policies. Per diem paid at or below GSA rates is generally not taxable income.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is per diem?

Per diem (Latin for 'per day') is a daily allowance for travel expenses including lodging, meals, and incidental expenses. The GSA sets per diem rates for federal travel within the continental US (CONUS), and many private employers use these rates as well.

What does M&IE include?

M&IE stands for Meals and Incidental Expenses. It covers breakfast, lunch, dinner, and incidentals such as tips to porters, baggage carriers, and hotel staff. M&IE does not cover transportation, laundry, or other non-meal expenses.

Is per diem taxable?

Per diem paid at or below GSA rates is generally not taxable income for employees. However, if your employer pays more than the GSA rate, the excess is taxable. Self-employed individuals can deduct actual expenses or use per diem rates for meals only.

Do I get full per diem on travel days?

The first and last day of travel are typically reimbursed at 75% of the M&IE rate. Full lodging is provided for nights you're away. Some employers reimburse full per diem for all days regardless.

How often do GSA per diem rates change?

GSA updates per diem rates annually, effective October 1 of each year (the start of the federal fiscal year). Rates are based on average daily rate data from lodging industry surveys. Some high-cost areas are adjusted more frequently.

Can I keep unused per diem money?

If your employer provides a flat per diem allowance, you can keep the difference if you spend less. If your employer requires receipts for actual expenses, you can only claim what you spent up to the per diem limit. Federal employees follow specific accountability rules.
