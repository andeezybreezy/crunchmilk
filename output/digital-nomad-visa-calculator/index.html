Frequently Asked Questions

What is a digital nomad visa?

A digital nomad visa is a special residence permit allowing remote workers to live in a foreign country while working for employers or clients based elsewhere. Unlike tourist visas, they explicitly authorize remote work, typically last 6-24 months, and often provide access to local banking, healthcare, and tax registration.

Do digital nomads pay taxes in the host country?

It varies significantly. Some countries (Barbados, Bermuda, Cayman Islands, Georgia) offer full tax exemption. Others (Portugal, Spain, Italy) have special reduced tax regimes. Some tax you after 183 days of residency. US citizens are taxed on worldwide income regardless — but may qualify for the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion ($126,500 in 2024).

What is the cheapest digital nomad visa?

Georgia has no visa needed for most nationalities (1-year stay, no income requirement). Mexico offers a 6-month tourist visa for free. Colombia's digital nomad visa is about $100. Among paid visas, Costa Rica ($100), Croatia ($100), and Estonia ($100) are affordable. Bermuda ($263) and Barbados ($2,000) are pricier.

Which country is best for digital nomads?

Popular choices include Portugal (great lifestyle, EU access, NHR tax regime), Thailand (low cost, excellent infrastructure), Colombia (affordable, vibrant cities), and Estonia (e-Residency, EU access). The best choice depends on your budget, timezone needs, lifestyle preferences, and tax situation.

Do I need health insurance for a digital nomad visa?

Almost all digital nomad visas require proof of health insurance valid in the destination country. Some accept global health insurance plans (SafetyWing, World Nomads), while others require local coverage. Coverage minimums vary — EU countries typically require more comprehensive coverage than Latin American or Asian destinations.

Can I bring my family on a digital nomad visa?

Most digital nomad visa programs allow dependents (spouse/partner and children) to accompany the primary applicant. Income requirements may increase — typically an extra $500-$1,500/month per dependent. Some countries (Portugal, Spain, Italy) provide dependent access to local schools and healthcare.
